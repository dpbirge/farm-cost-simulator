LCOW: Levelized Cost of Water ($0.46/m³)

Water cost is the dominant variable cost for irrigated agriculture in eastern Sinai. Unlike the Nile Valley and Delta, where farmers pay nothing for raw irrigation water (bearing only on-farm pumping costs), eastern Sinai has no access to the Nile system. The Al-Salam Canal serves parts of northern Sinai west of El-Arish with mixed Nile/drainage water, but does not extend to eastern Sinai near the Gulf of Aqaba. Farmers must source water independently, and there is no published government tariff for agricultural desalinated water in Sinai.

What farmers actually pay depends on the water source:

The default of $0.46/m³ reflects a favorable brackish groundwater scenario with existing solar pumping infrastructure and short distribution distances. This sits at the optimistic end of the feasible range but is supported by literature showing $0.55-0.63/m³ OPEX for solar-powered brackish RO in Egypt.

Desalination infrastructure in North Sinai: The El-Arish desalination plant (100,000 m³/day Phase 1, expanding to 300,000 m³/day, $97M capital cost) is under commissioning as of late 2025 for municipal drinking water supply to ~350,000 residents. Nine additional desalination plants serve Rafah, Sheikh Zuweid, El-Arish, and Bir El-Abd. There is currently no indication that municipal desalinated water will be allocated for agricultural use at subsidized rates.

Exchange rate context: EGP-denominated water costs are a moving target. The Egyptian pound lost ~70% of its value against USD between early 2022 and late 2024 through three sequential devaluations. LCOE: Levelized Cost of Energy ($0.08/kWh)

Energy for agricultural pumping in eastern Sinai reflects multiple options, but most Sinai farms lack grid connection, making off-grid diesel or solar the relevant cost basis. Egypt's electricity subsidy reform program has raised agricultural tariffs from ~65 pt/kWh (FY 2019/20) to 200 pt/kWh (Sep 2024), with a further 25% hike proposed for late 2025. However, in USD terms the grid tariff has actually fallen due to EGP devaluation — from ~$0.039/kWh in 2019 to ~$0.039/kWh in 2024 at the same rate in USD despite tripling in EGP.

Diesel price trajectory: EGP 6.75/L (2019) to EGP 17.50/L (Oct 2025). At 0.3 L/kWh genset consumption, diesel-only LCOE in remote Sinai has risen from ~$0.15/kWh to ~$0.17/kWh in USD. IFC estimates converting Egypt's ~960,000 diesel pumps to solar could save farmers EGP 14 billion (~$275M) annually.

RO desalination energy requirements: The simulator's pumping energy parameter (0.10 kWh/m³) represents well-pumping only. If RO desalination is required, specific energy consumption (SEC) adds 0.5-4.2 kWh/m³ depending on feed water salinity (1,000-23,000 ppm). North Sinai's El-Arish aquifer averages ~2,800 ppm TDS, implying 1.5-3.0 kWh/m³ for brackish RO treatment.

The default of $0.08/kWh sits in the middle of the solar+diesel hybrid range, which is the most likely energy source for a Sinai farm without grid access. Egypt's solar irradiance (2,000-2,400 kWh/m²/year in Sinai) makes solar particularly favorable.

Sources: EgyptERA tariff schedules (Sep 2024, Jan 2024); IRENA Renewable Power Generation Costs 2024 ($0.043/kWh global solar PV); Energypedia "Solar Powered Irrigation Systems in Egypt"; IFC + Agricultural Bank of Egypt solar pump partnership (2020); IFC + ALEXBANK partnership (2021); Benban Solar Park (1.8 GW operational); Springer solar PV pumping optimization Egypt (2025); Africa Oil & Gas Report (diesel prices, Apr 2025); Al Manassa (25% electricity hike, Oct 2025)

LCOI: Levelized Cost of Irrigation ($500/ha per season)

LCOI represents the amortized per-hectare per-season cost of the drip irrigation system: drip tape or lines, filtration, fertigation injectors, mainlines, submains, fittings, and installation labor. Capital costs for a 1-hectare open-field tomato drip system in Eastern Sinai are estimated at $2,800-3,500/ha installed — 20-40% above Nile Delta averages due to transport logistics, enhanced filtration needs, and limited local suppliers.

Water quality impact: Eastern Sinai's brackish groundwater (1,500-5,000 ppm TDS) with high calcium and bicarbonate content accelerates emitter clogging by 2-3x vs Nile water. Thin-wall tape may last only 1 season. Acid flushing (phosphoric/nitric) every 2-4 weeks is required, adding $30-80/ha/season in maintenance chemicals. This adds an estimated $80-150/ha/season to LCOI vs sweet-water regions.

Labor Rate ($0.75/hr)

Agricultural labor in eastern Sinai is priced in the context of Egypt's informal agricultural economy, where 50-60% of employment operates outside formal wage structures. Egypt's first explicit hourly minimum for agricultural workers was set at EGP 28/hr (~$0.56/hr) effective March 2025 — a landmark, as agriculture had been largely exempt from prior minimum wage legislation. Enforcement in Sinai remains limited given security conditions and informal arrangements.

Exchange rate effect: Nominal EGP wages have roughly tripled since 2019 (EGP 100-150/day to EGP 350-500/day), but USD-equivalent wages have remained remarkably stable in the $0.75-1.25/hr band. Each EGP devaluation temporarily compresses USD wages, then nominal wages catch up within 6-12 months. The default of $0.75/hr represents the post-devaluation floor of this band.

Fertilizer Price ($0.55/kg blended)

Fertilizer costs are based on the FAO-recommended 300-110-115 kg/ha of N-P&sub2;O&sub5;-K&sub2;O for tomatoes at 450 kg/ha total application. Egypt is a major producer of nitrogen (Abu Qir, MOPCO) and phosphate fertilizers, but imports all potash (SOP/MOP) from Jordan, Belarus/Russia, and Canada. The K&sub2;O component via SOP represents 40-50% of total fertilizer cost despite being only ~25% of nutrient weight.

Subsidy access in Sinai: The Ministry of Agriculture allocates subsidized N fertilizer (30-50% below market) through cooperatives, but new/reclaimed lands in Sinai have limited access. Quota is typically 2-3 bags (50 kg) per feddan per season — a fraction of total N needs. No subsidy on imported potash or SOP. Net effect: 10-20% total cost reduction at best, vs 25-35% for Nile Delta farmers.

The default of $0.55/kg sits in the middle of the $0.38-0.62 range for a tomato-specific blend delivered to Eastern Sinai. Global fertilizer prices have largely retreated from 2022 peaks, but EGP devaluation (from ~16 to ~50 per USD) means Egyptian farmers pay 2-3x more in local currency.

Seed Price ($2,000/kg seed)

Egypt's vegetable seed market is ~75-80% import-dependent, dominated by Seminis/Bayer, Syngenta, and HM Clause. Most Egyptian farmers buy seed in packets of 1,000 or 5,000 seeds rather than by the kilogram. At ~300,000-350,000 seeds/kg for tomato, a typical planting density of 22,000-28,000 seeds/ha (accounting for nursery losses) means 0.07-0.08 kg of raw seed per hectare. The simulator uses a 0.4 kg/ha seeding rate which is higher, accounting for nursery and field losses.

Sinai variety requirements: Arish University and Desert Research Center trials indicate F1 hybrids with TYLCV resistance are essential for Sinai (whitefly-transmitted virus is endemic). Heat-set capability (fruit set above 35°C) and salt-tolerant rootstocks (e.g., Maxifort, adding ~$0.15-0.25/seedling) are recommended for brackish groundwater areas.

The default of $2,000/kg corresponds to open-pollinated or locally-multiplied seed, yielding ~$800/ha at 0.4 kg/ha. For F1 hybrids (which most commercial growers use), $/kg is much higher but the per-hectare cost is similar ($660-1,680/ha) because less seed is needed. EGP devaluation tripled imported seed costs in local currency terms between 2019-2025, but USD prices were relatively stable with 10-20% supply chain friction premiums.

Packaging & Shipping ($0.05/kg)

Packaging and shipping covers farm-gate-to-market costs. Standard domestic packaging in Egypt uses reusable plastic crates (20 kg capacity), with amortized packaging costs of $0.01-0.03/kg. Transport is the dominant component, estimated from World Bank developing-country road freight benchmarks ($0.04-0.13/ton-km) adjusted for Egypt's diesel price of EGP 20.5/liter (March 2026). Eastern Sinai is ~30 km from El-Arish, ~200 km from Ismailia, and ~350 km from Cairo's Obour wholesale market. Tomatoes in Egypt's domestic market are transported without refrigeration (only ~30% cold chain coverage nationally). Total costs range from $0.02/kg for local El-Arish sales to $0.09/kg for Cairo wholesale delivery. The default of $0.05/kg reflects a mix of local and regional market sales.

Sources: FAO Food Loss Analysis: Tomato Value Chains in Egypt (2021, 13.4% post-harvest losses); World Bank road freight benchmarks; GlobalPetrolPrices Egypt diesel; Tridge/SelinaWamucii Egypt wholesale tomato prices; FreshPlaza (45% packaging cost increase, 2024)

Pest & Disease Control ($350/ha)

Pest and disease control material costs cover insecticides, fungicides, biological control agents, adjuvants, and pheromone traps -- excluding labor, which is accounted for separately (50 hrs/ha for scouting, spraying, and IPM). The default of $350/ha per season reflects a moderate integrated pest management (IPM) approach suitable for a small community farm in eastern Sinai. Key pests include Tuta absoluta (tomato leaf miner), which increased Mediterranean tomato production costs by over 450 EUR/ha when it first invaded; Bemisia tabaci (whitefly), the primary vector of tomato yellow leaf curl virus (TYLCV); and spider mites, which thrive in Sinai's hot, dry conditions. Sinai's arid climate (<100 mm annual rainfall) significantly reduces fungal disease pressure compared to the Nile Delta, shifting the cost balance toward insecticides (~70%) over fungicides (~20%) and biocontrol/adjuvants (~10%). A typical spray program involves 8-12 insecticide applications and 3-5 fungicide applications per season. Reference costs: Turkish open-field tomato at $141/ha; West African conventional at $285/ha (reduced to $85/ha under IPM); Kenyan smallholder at $300/ha (reduced to $179/ha with biocontrol).

Additional Costs ($15,000/ha)

A flat per-cycle catchall for costs not itemized elsewhere in the simulator, such as land rent or lease payments, insurance, equipment depreciation (tractors, sprayers, tools), soil preparation and bed forming, mulch film, crop support structures, management overhead, financing costs, and government fees or permits. The range of $10,000-$20,000/ha reflects the wide variability in these costs depending on land tenure arrangements, equipment ownership vs. rental, and farm scale.

Calculation Chain

  1. Daily Kc schedule built from 4 growth stages (initial, development, mid, late)
  2. Daily Kc assigned to calendar months starting from planting month
  3. Monthly ETc = avg_Kc x daily_ET0 x days_in_month_occupied
  4. Monthly water (m³/ha) = ETc_mm x 10 / irrigation_efficiency
  5. Total water = sum of monthly water across growing season
  6. Water cost = LCOW x total_water
  7. Irrigation cost = LCOI (flat $/ha per cycle)
  8. Energy cost = LCOE x kWh/m³ x total_water
  9. Labor cost = labor_rate x labor_hours/ha
  10. Fertilizer cost = fert_price x fert_kg/ha
  11. Seed cost = seed_price x seed_kg/ha
  12. Total cost/ha = sum of above
  13. Cost/kg (ex. packaging) = total_cost/ha / yield_kg/ha
  14. Cost/kg (delivered) = cost/kg + packaging_shipping
  15. Profit/ha = (market_price - cost/kg) x yield_kg/ha
